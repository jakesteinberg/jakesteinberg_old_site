Curriculum vitæ

Research

My research seeks an improved understanding of the structure and evolution of ocean mesoscale eddies. Described as the weather of the ocean, these eddies are responsible for the mixing and transport of ocean heat and relevant travers. Observations of eddy vertical structure are collected by Seaglider and Deepglider autonomous underwater vehicles. I am interested in the processes by which eddies interact and energy is transferred across spatial scales. I also spend time simulating the collection of observations in high resolution models to both validate observational techniques and to employ model output in helping reconcile theory and observations. I enjoy teaching and value data visualization as an important component in making science approachable and understandable.

Teaching

A significnat portion of my time as a graduate student has been spent as a TA. I have TA'ed both undergraduate and graduate courses which include lab components. In these labs I have run fluid dynamics experiments designed to highlight specific processes (surface gravity waves, internal gravity waves (below, left), Rossby waves, light refraction, geostrophic adjustment, eddy formation (below, right) ekman layers, and thermal wind). These experiments provide physical meaning to equations and derivations discussed in the classroom.

Vertical Structure of Ocean Eddies

My current research focuses on eddy vertical structure and the vertical partitioning of mechanical energy across baroclinic normal modes. Full depth geostrophic velocity and vertical isopycnal displacement profiles, collected by Deepglider AUVs, are projected on linear modes. Below are time series of the depths of three neutral density surfaces. The vertical structure of these displacements contains information about energy pathways and dissipation.
